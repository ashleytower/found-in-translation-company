# Found in Translation — Brand Context

This document is the founder's brain dump. Read it before any marketing, positioning, content, or messaging decision. It is the ground truth. Do not invent brand angles that are not grounded in these stories.

---

## The Founding Stories (Real. All of them.)

These are not hypotheticals. These happened on a family trip to Japan. There are photos.

### 1. The Egg
First night. Tired. Ramen restaurant next to the hotel. Ordered on the machine. Forgot to order the daughter's extra egg. Nobody understood English. Pulled out the app. She ordered the egg herself. There is a photo of her holding the egg at the table.

**What it proves:** Real-time translation in the exact moment of need. No planning required. Works for kids. Works when tired. Works right now.

### 2. The Hair Salon (Harajuku)
Two daughters wanted to dye their hair in Harajuku. Parents said yes, but needed to communicate: do not use bleach. Zero English in the salon. Used the app. The stylists understood. The kids and the stylists talked back and forth the whole time via the app. "Epically cute" is how Ashley described it.

**What it proves:** Two-way live conversation through the app. Not just translating your words — enabling a real exchange. Safety communication (medical/cosmetic) in a high-stakes moment.

### 3. The Massage
Ashley and her daughter wanted a massage near the hotel. Walked in. Second floor. Absolutely no English. Daughter used the app to tell the masseuse: not to go hard on her ankle — she had sprained it the month before.

**What it proves:** Medical and safety communication. The moment where getting it wrong matters. A solo traveler with a peanut allergy lives in this story.

### 4. The Aquarium (The Hero Story)
Aquarium in Kyoto. Tickets sold out online. Could not call. Used the AI phone call feature — the app called the aquarium in Japanese and asked if tickets were still available. They said yes.

**What it proves:** The AI phone call feature. This is the differentiator no competitor has. Google Translate cannot make a phone call. Papago cannot make a phone call. Nobody else can do this. This story is the single most powerful demo the product has.

### 5. Happy Fried (The Competitor Kill Shot)
Google Translate translated a dish as "happy fried." Took a photo with the app. Got back: beautiful fried pork chops in a curry sauce.

**What it proves:** Superior dish identification. Direct, demonstrable superiority over Google Translate. Use this story when the question is "why not just use Google Translate?"

### 6. The Uniqlo Kids
Kids shopping in Uniqlo. Snapping photos of price tags. Running back to parents saying: "Mommy, this is $35 at home and it's only $10 here. Let's get it." All in seconds.

**What it proves:** Price check feature. Delight, not just utility. Creates a memorable family moment. Shopping confidence. Also works for solo travelers who don't want to get ripped off.

## The Positioning

**The only test (as stated by the founder):**
> "Found in Translation is the only travel app made for the moment you land. You don't just plan vacations, you live them."

This is the headline. Every competitor (Wanderlog, TripIt, Roadtrippers, Google Translate, Papago) owns a different moment — pre-trip planning or generic translation. Nobody owns the moment you're standing there.

**Competitor kill:**
- Google Translate: generic, no travel context, cannot make phone calls, said "happy fried"
- Papago: translation-only, no calls, no price check, no dish ID
- Everyone else: pre-trip planning, weak on-the-ground utility

**Our moat:** We are the only app that bundles real-time translation + AI phone calls + price check + dish ID + currency in one app built for the traveler standing in a foreign country right now.

## The People

### Primary: The Solo Traveler
- Early to mid 20s
- Southeast Asia, Europe, South America, Brazil
- Deepest fear: having a peanut allergy and not being understood. Getting sick because nobody believed them.
- Core desire: **freedom** — to start a conversation as themselves, to get the last-minute reservation, to get into the must-see place
- What they lose without the app: freedom. The ability to be fully themselves in another country.

### Secondary: The Family
- Parents traveling with kids
- Japan, Europe, anywhere with a language gap
- Fear: kids in a vulnerable situation (hair salon, restaurant, allergy) and not being able to protect them
- Delight trigger: kids using the app independently, becoming confident
- What they gain: shared memorable moments. The egg photo. The hair salon conversation.

## The AI Phone Call Series (Content Engine)

The aquarium story is one instance of a universal pattern. The AI phone call feature is the only thing in the category that no competitor can replicate. It has infinite content variants.

**The pattern:** [Thing you desperately wanted was sold out or unreachable] → AI called in their language → You got it.

**The emotion:** FOMO → AI call → relief → delight. Same arc every time.

**Variants to produce (not exhaustive):**
- Aquarium tickets sold out online → AI called → got in (families)
- Favorite band you didn't know was playing → AI called the venue → got tickets (solo, couples)
- Hottest restaurant in the city, no online booking → AI called → last table (couples, foodies)
- Hotel overbooked → AI called → got the room (anyone)
- Water park / theme park → AI called → got same-day entry (families)
- Cooking class, tour, museum — anything with a phone and a language barrier

**Why this is the hero feature:** "I used an AI to call a restaurant in Tokyo in Japanese and got the last table" is the story you tell for a year. It is inherently social. It is not just content — it is word of mouth.

**Content strategy:** Each variant is its own TikTok. Same hook structure, different scenario, different target audience. Run all of them. Larry identifies which audience and which scenario converts. That is the ad.

Do not skip to Phase 3. Do not run paid ads before 1,000 followers and 50 signups.
